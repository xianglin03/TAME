Response to Reviewer

Thank you for raising these points. We agree that our original discussion of DP-SGD was not sufficiently clear, and that the presentation in the current table was potentially confusing because it reported MLE/MEM and DQ/MEM, rather than raw MLE and raw DQ. In the revision, we will (i) keep DP-SGD as an explicit baseline, (ii) report the full DP-SGD protocol ([same backbone / preprocessing / split as TAME], [optimizer, clipping, accountant], and exact privacy budget [, (iii) add results across multiple privacy budgets, and (iv) include additional downstream evaluations on non-label targets.

1. On whether DP-SGD is a relevant baseline, and on the conceptual role of sampling-time privacy.
We agree that DP-SGD is a natural baseline and should not be dismissed. Our intention was not to argue that comparison to DP-SGD is “unfair,” but rather that training-time DP and sampling-time privacy operate at different points of the pipeline. Training-time DP perturbs optimization, whereas sampling-time privacy constrains the release mechanism at generation time. This is practically relevant when the generator is already trained, when retraining with DP-SGD is infeasible or costly, or when one wants to enforce privacy at release time rather than during optimization. We will revise the paper to make this distinction explicit and to present DP-SGD as a complementary baseline rather than an inappropriate one.

We also agree that our original table did not provide the right evidence for the claim about utility degradation, because the ratio MLE/MEM can be unstable when MEM is very small. The absolute numbers are more informative. For the setting in question, the raw metrics are:

DP-SGD: MEM = 0.0503, MLE = 0.261, DQ = 0.163
TAME: MEM = 0.1381, MLE = 0.587, DQ = 0.743

Thus, DP-SGD has lower memorization but also substantially lower utility in this setting; the larger value of MLE/MEM is driven by the very small MEM denominator, not by higher raw MLE. We will therefore de-emphasize ratio-only comparisons and replace them with absolute metrics and a privacy–utility trade-off plot in the revision.

2. On the experimental protocol and comparison across privacy budgets.
We agree that the DP-SGD benchmark was under-specified. In the revision, we will explicitly state that the DP-SGD baseline uses [the same backbone / data split / preprocessing as TAME], with [exact training and privacy-accounting details], and we will report the exact privacy budget for each run ([fill in exact 

We additionally ran DP-SGD at four privacy settings:

[budget 1]: MEM = 0.036, MLE = 0.145, DQ = 0.249
[budget 4]: MEM = 0.0424, MLE = 0.227, DQ = 0.288
[budget 8]: MEM = 0.0503, MLE = 0.261, DQ = 0.163
[budget 16]: MEM = 0.0933, MLE = 0.351, DQ = 0.310

For reference, our sampling-only baseline obtains MEM = 0.1405, MLE = 0.645, DQ = 0.673, and TAME obtains MEM = 0.1381, MLE = 0.587, DQ = 0.743.

Across the DP-SGD privacy settings we evaluated, utility remains substantially below TAME. At the same time, we agree that these results do not show strict Pareto dominance of TAME on both utility and memorization, and we will revise the claim accordingly. Our revised conclusion will be narrower and more precise: TAME provides a substantially stronger utility operating point, while DP-SGD remains an important baseline and may be preferable when minimizing memorization is the primary objective.

1. On other downstream targets beyond the label column.
We have added an additional comparison on non-label downstream targets. On shoppers, using non-label columns as targets, TAME remains much closer to real-data performance than DP-SGD:

Categorical non-label target (F1): Real = 0.3533, DP-SGD = 0.2812, TAME = 0.3236
Continuous non-label target (R²): Real = 0.5741, DP-SGD = -4.6641, TAME = 0.5302

These new results suggest that the benefit of TAME is not limited to the label column, and more directly support the claim that TAME better preserves cross-attribute structure on downstream tasks involving non-label attributes as well. We will add these results to the paper and clarify that our claim is not that DP-SGD is irrelevant, but that sampling-time privacy offers a different and practically useful trade-off, especially in settings where retraining with DP-SGD is difficult.
